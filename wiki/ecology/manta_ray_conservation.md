# Manta Ray Conservation

## Summary
Manta rays are threatened globally by commercial fisheries and require coordinated international conservation efforts. There are 10 species of manta and devil rays that depend on healthy marine ecosystems for survival.

## Key Facts
- 10 species of manta and devil rays exist worldwide
- Commercial fisheries represent the primary threat
- Conservation requires global affiliate networks and collaboration
- Research and monitoring are essential to understanding population status
- Education and policy support are key conservation tools
- Reef manta rays feed on zooplankton concentrated by bay currents and tidal patterns
- More than 250 individual manta rays have been identified at Hanifaru Bay
- Manta rays are vulnerable to warming oceans that reduce tropical zooplankton biomass
- Field research uses freediving-based plankton sampling to minimize disturbance
- Manta Trust conducts ongoing research to understand feeding ecology and environmental factors
- Manta rays are most frequently encountered August–November during the nutrient-rich monsoon season
- Cleaning stations are dedicated reef sites where manta rays interact with wrasse and shrimp for parasite removal
- Manta rays are attracted to drift diving sites along atoll walls and channels where currents bring food
- Manta encounters are a major attraction for liveaboard and resort-based dive operations in the Maldives
- Large congregations of up to 100 manta rays documented at Hanifaru Bay
- Feed on plankton concentrated by tidal action in shallow bays
- Protected within Baa Atoll Biosphere Reserve's core protection zones
- Major draw for sustainable marine tourism in the Maldives
