# Maldivian Cuisine

## Summary
Maldivian cuisine is heavily based on fish and coconut, reflecting the island nation's maritime environment and agricultural resources. Traditional food practices are central to local culture.

## Key Facts
- Fish is a primary staple of Maldivian cuisine
- Coconut is widely used in traditional cooking
- Cuisine reflects the island's maritime and agricultural heritage
- Food traditions are shaped by island life and local resources
- Fish and coconut are the foundational ingredients of Maldivian cuisine
- Mas Huni with Roshi is a signature breakfast staple
- Garudhiya and Baiy are common everyday meals
- Common side dishes include fried fish, boiled yam, sweet potatoes, and Theluli Faiy
- Snacks and sweets include Bondibai, Kulhi Boakibaa, Bajiyaa, and Mas Roshi
- Maldivian food is available in both local islands and resort dining establishments
- The cuisine reflects influences from neighboring countries and historical seafaring trade routes
- Primary ingredients include sustainably caught tuna, coconut, spices, starches, grains, and vegetables
- Available across all accommodation types including resorts, guesthouses, hotels, and liveaboards
- Commonly offered as special buffet options or upon request, with vegetarian, vegan, and gluten-free variants
- Traditional drinks include toddy, sour mango juice, bilimbi juice, seasonal kashikeyo juice, and jasmine-infused black tea
- Signature dishes include Mashuni, Garudhiya and Baiy, Masbaiy, Baiypen, Kulhi Baiypen, Kiru boakiba, Bondibai, and Huni Hakuru
